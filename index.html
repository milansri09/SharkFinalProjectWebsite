Introduction

Amongst the great diversity in life found here on planet Earth, there are few animals as alluring as the shark. Forever beholden to a place in the pop culture lexicon as terrifying beings of raw carnage through movies such as “Jaws” or the “Sharknado” film series, it is actually the case that these majestic creatures are more nuanced than just serving as mindless killing machines. In fact, sharks are one of the oldest and most successful animals on Earth, surviving for hundreds of millions of years through dramatic environmental changes and multiple mass extinction events, giving them a richer history than even the dinosaurs themselves. Their survival is due to their remarkable adaptability, specialized anatomy, and ecological diversity, which allowed them to persist while countless other species simply faded away to the annals of history. While pop science tends to simply classify sharks as “living fossils”, a deeper insight reveals that sharks have actually continued to evolve into many different forms over time, demonstrating how adaptation and diversity can ensure survival across Earth’s changes.

Background

Due to the cartilaginous makeup of a shark’s anatomy that grants them their speed under water, it means that there is no bone to be fossilized, but instead, paleontologists have been able to find scales, vertebrae, and even some tissue impressions. These pale in comparison, however, to shark teeth, which completely dominate the shark fossil record. Sharks, unlike humans, continuously discard and replace their teeth throughout their life, which adds up to thousands over time per shark and making it the most common fossil found today. Through these, scientists can study ancient diets, habitats, evolutionary relationships, size, and feeding behavior. In fact, according to the Smithsonian, the existing fossil evidence for sharks has revealed “more than 2,000 species of fossil sharks have been described” (Shiffman, 2018), with more waiting to be found.

History

450 Million Years Ago

Late Ordovician

The earliest evidence of a primitive shark-like creature can be traced back here according to the Natural History Museum of London, with scales suggesting that this creature existed before trees first appeared on land (Davis, 2018). From this early ancestor, scientists believe two distinct groups emerged: bony fish and cartilaginous fish, the latter including sharks, rays, skates, and chimaeras. The earliest version of a shark was much different from what “Shark Week” shows today.

400 Million Years Ago

Devonian Period

Primitive sharks still lacked most traditional hallmarks of modern shark species but began to develop streamlined bodies for faster swimming and sharper teeth for hunting. With the various aquatic environments available, sharks rapidly diversified and adapted to the world around them.

359 Million Years Ago

The Golden Age of Sharks - Carboniferous Period

After the Devonian Period ended with a mass extinction wiping out most competing fish groups, sharks could hail as masters of the aquatic realm. Despite still lacking the movie star physique of modern sharks like the Great White, the open oceans of this era were positively teeming with shark diversity that resulted in many unique and specialized species. This was due to sharks experimenting with different body types and feeding strategies to make use of their environment and newfound opportunities, giving some interesting features like the anvil-shaped dorsal fin of the Stethacanthus or the spiral buzzsaw jaw in Helicoprion.

252 Million Years Ago

Permian-Triassic Extinction

Alas, all good things must come to an end eventually. Earth's largets mass extinction wiped out most of Earth’s marine life. With many of these shark subspecies finding themselves eradicated, some managed to still survive and swim forward. Hailing the ecological flexibility of the shark, scientists from the Smithsonian highlight the shark’s comfort in various different ocean depths, ability to consume wide ranges of prey, and general diversity as key factors in their continued survival in the face of dramatic environmental changes (Shiffman, 2018).

Jurassic & Cretaceous

The Rise of Modern Sharks

We must note that it is here that sharks started to more closely resemble what many fear today (The Shark Trust, 2020). The emergence of flexible protruding jaws allowed them to capture larger prey, and improved swimming adaptations birthed faster and more efficient deep sea hunters. Around 195 million years ago, the beginnings of modern shark groups such as sixgill sharks began to appear with other groups such as ground sharks trailing behind and hammerheads eventually appearing as one of the very last modern groups, with fossil evidence suggesting that they appeared only around 23 million years ago.

23–3.6 Million Years Ago

Megalodon

As sharks become more “sharklike”, we find one of the most famous prehistoric sharks: the Megalodon (yes, from the Jason Statham movie). Evolving in the Paleogene Period after the dinosaurs ended their terrestrial reign, it was one of the largest predators to ever exist. According to the Natural History museum in London, it clocked in at over 20 meters long and preyed on whales and other large marine animals (Davis, 2018). Eventually, this king of the sharks went extinct around 3.6 million years ago from what scientists theorize could have been climate change, reduced prey ability, and increased competition.

Today

Modern Life

Sharks now inhabit nearly every marine environment on Earth, from coral reefs to Arctic waters and the deep ocean.

Final Musings

In today’s 21st century, the Earth is proud to call itself home to over 500 species of sharks around the world. They range from a wild spectrum of different sizes (the tiny dwarf lantern shark to the whale shark) and inhabit almost every single marine habitat there is (including but not limited to coral reefs, Arctic waters, and the deep ocean). On top of that, evolution has blessed them with advanced sense and an elite build to make them effective predators, yet they are not monsters. Simply animals following what natural selection and evolution have bred them to do, they now find themselves threatened by humanity. The Shark Trust notes that overfishing, habitat destruction, and shark finning have caused severe population declines worldwide, with some experts saying as many as 25% of current shark species are presently facing extinction.

From a time before trees to the time of the Internet, sharks have continuously demonstrated a ceaseless sense of survival, adaptation, and resilience. Despite staring down changing climates, a changing world, and mass extinction after mass extinction, sharks never let up in evolving into the next champion of the deep. With biodiversity as the greatest weapon in their arsenal, sharks have seen the world change a thousand times over. Still, with human actions bringing a brand new level of terror to sharks at a scale never seen before, one can only wonder if the species will survive to see a new era.
